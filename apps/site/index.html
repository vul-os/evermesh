Many gateways. One substrate. Video that outlives its platforms.

Vidmesh separates video into a small, permanent substrate of signed records and content-addressed blobs, and a competitive edge of gateways, nodes, and viewers that handle everything contested — moderation, discovery, monetization — by their own choice, not by protocol rule.

Three ways to take part

Participation is a ladder. Every rung is optional and useful on its own.

Viewer

Runs nothing but a browser or app. Verifies signatures and hashes client-side while watching, and quietly helps seed the swarm. No setup, no commitment.

Node

A small background app that pins content you choose — your own videos first, subscriptions second — and seeds it to others within budgets you set. No public-facing duties.

Gateway

An independent website on its own domain that indexes and serves its own selection of the substrate. Sets its own moderation policy, handles its own jurisdiction and compliance, and competes on product — search, transcoding, community, payment.

A creator is not a separate infrastructure role — it is any keyholder who publishes video manifests. Creators may also run their own node and gateway. A foundation stewards the specification, registries, and conformance suite, and runs no servers of its own.

Design principles

Ten rules the protocol is not allowed to break, condensed from the spec overview.

  1. Minimal kernel. Only the record envelope, algorithm registry, identity rotation log, and blob addressing are permanent. Everything else is an extension.
  2. Self-certifying. Every record and blob verifies from its own bytes and math — no server, chain, DNS name, or certificate required.
  3. Forkability. The full index can be replicated by anyone; identities are portable; blobs are re-hostable by anyone who has them.
  4. Transport and storage agnostic. The kernel never names a transport. Blobs are hashes; delivery details are optional hints.
  5. No mandatory dependencies. No company, blockchain, or external network is required for the protocol to work correctly.
  6. Economic neutrality. Payment is optional and pluggable. There is no protocol token, and there will not be one.
  7. Edge-resolved moderation. The substrate never deletes. Gateways choose what to serve; compliance is plural, subscribable, signed takedown feeds.
  8. Partition tolerance. No part of the protocol needs global consensus or ordering. It keeps working across slow links, offline stretches, or permanent isolation.
  9. Two-implementations rule. Nothing joins the specification until two independent implementations interoperate against the public conformance suite.
  10. Legibility. Formats are plain, documented, and boring on purpose, so a stranger can reimplement the whole thing from the spec alone.

The specification

Twelve files, one concern each, cross-referenced and self-consistent. Code follows spec — never the reverse.

  1. 000-overview — principles, roles, and the map of the spec
  2. 001-kernel — record envelope, canonical CBOR, ids, algorithm registry, blobs, chunk trees
  3. 002-identity — rotation log, recovery precedence, delegation, profiles
  4. 003-kinds-registry — the numeric registry of record kinds and their schemas
  5. 004-manifest — video manifests, renditions, deduplication, live streams
  6. 005-claims — authorship and license claims, disputes, provenance
  7. 006-relay — relay sync protocol, blob sidecar, anti-spam, gossip
  8. 007-bundles — the offline bundle container format and partition posture
  9. 008-privacy — encryption modes, key grants, metadata privacy
  10. 009-gateway — gateway selection, compliance, legal toolkit, discovery
  11. 010-economics — payment pointers, receipts, disclosures
  12. 011-threat-model — adversaries, attack surfaces, mitigations, residual risk

Frequently asked questions

How is this different from PeerTube, Nostr, or Bluesky?
PeerTube federates independent servers, but each server still owns the identity and data of its own users — moving off one means starting over. Vidmesh keeps identity and content self-certifying and portable; gateways select and serve rather than own.

Nostr shares the same substrate spirit — signed events distributed through relays you choose. Vidmesh is built specifically for video on top of that idea: native manifests, renditions, authorship claims, and offline bundles that a plain event feed does not define.

Bluesky / the AT Protocol also uses rotation-based, portable identity, similar in spirit to Vidmesh's identity log. The difference is that Vidmesh has no required global relay or app-view service — every gateway can build its own view from scratch, and the protocol is designed to keep working when partitioned, not just when everyone can reach a central firehose.
Is there a token?
No, permanently. The specification carries optional payment primitives so creators and gateways can charge for things if they want to, but it takes no position on business models, and no protocol token exists or will exist.
Is this a blockchain?
No. There is no chain, no mining, and no global consensus of any kind. Records are signed and content-addressed, which is enough to make them independently verifiable — that verification never depends on everyone agreeing on a shared ledger.
What happens if a gateway shuts down?
Nothing is lost. The records and blobs it served are just as valid on any other gateway that chooses to index them, and any node that pinned the underlying blobs can re-seed them. Identity and history live in the substrate, not in the gateway.
Who is in charge?
No one operationally. A foundation stewards the specification, the kind registry, and the conformance suite, but it runs no servers and holds no user data. The design goal is that the network keeps working even after the foundation is gone.
